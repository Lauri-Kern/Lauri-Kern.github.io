What is the motivation for metasurface holography?
  1. Metasurfaces provide a platform for manipulating the wavefront of output light within ultrashort distance in subwavelength resolution
  2. The cool thing about that is it can be used for scientific as well as industrial applications
What are metasurfaces?
  1. Metasurfaces consisting of arrays of plasmonic or dielectric nanoantennas these units are called meta-atoms. (-> see summary table for Haoran)
  2. Dielectric nanoantennas are cool because of their ability to arbitrarily tailor the fundamental properties of the electromagnetic wavefront within ultrashort distance such as:
    1. Amplitude
    2. Phase
    3. Frequency
    4. Polarization
    5. Orbital angular momentum (OAM)

How do metasurfaces work?

Intro to beam shaping
  1. In classical optics, light (EM-wave) is shaped using waveplates or lenses (for example to make devices such as microscopes/telescopes)
  2. Beam shaping in traditional optical elements means changing an existing wavefront of light i.e. spatial amplitude / phase profile
  3. Problem: Thickness of optical elements > wavelength of incident light → not useful in compact integrated optical systems.
  4. Solution: With the development of nanofabrication technologies, flat, ultrathin optical components (metasurfaces) can manipulate the amplitude, phase, polarization, frequency or simultaneously multiple parameters of output light in ultrashort distances with subwavelength resolution by tailoring the scattering behaviour of consisted nanostructures
  5. Metasurfaces are created by assembling arrays of miniature, anisotropic light scatterers (resonators such as optical antennas)
  6. The spacing between antennas and their dimensions are much smaller than the wavelength → the metasurfaces, on account of Huygens principle, are able to mould optical wavefronts into arbitrary shapes with subwavelength resolution by introducing spatial variations in the optical response of the light scatterers.
  7. Why is it cool to arbitrarily tailor the wavefront of an electromagnetic wave?
    Answer: there are many applications in transformation optics and integrated optics (Pick 2 or 3 examples)
    1. Fundamental studies of light-matter interactions [1–5]
    2. Photonic applications in optical microscopy and imaging [6–9]
    3. Meta-holograms for holographic displays [21–24]

Photopolymers in Holography
Why use photopolymers in holography?
  1. Photopolymers are used in holographic applications due to their higher diffraction efficiency with an acceptable energetic sensitivity.
  2. They are easily made at a reduced cost and have great flexibility as holographic recording materials
What are photopolymers?
  1. A photopolymer is an organic polymer sensitive to the light of certain wavelength
  2. The incorporation of liquid crystals enables the variation of the electrooptical properties by means of an electric field
  3. Liquid crystal molecules add optical anisotropy to the photopolymer - therefore it is possible to change the photopolymer response by modifying the electric field applied
  4. The orientation of the liquid crystal produces a refractive index variation which changes the diffraction efficiency.
How do photopolymers work?
  1. The basic formula is made of
    1. sensitizer dye
    2. an initiator to generate free radicals
    3. one or more polymerizable monomers
  2. To record a diffractive optical element into a photopolymer (a phase media) is by refractive index modulation between the polymerized and non polymerized areas, which correspond to illuminated and nonilluminated areas, respectively
  3. The photopolymerization reaction (Figure 1) starts when the photopolymers are illuminated by light of certain wavelength, and the monomer starts to polymerize, causing a shrinkage on the illuminated areas, increasing the refractive index and decreasing the monomer concentration of these areas.
  4. This chemical compound gradient of the monomer, dye and radical generator causes a diffusion of the different components from the areas where the concentration is high, “dark areas” to the areas where the concentration has decreased, the “illuminated areas.”
  5. This diffusion causes a decreasing of volume in the dark areas, and therefore, the volume of the illuminated areas is increased by incoming molecules, counteracting the shrinkage due to the polymerization process.
